# LAUNCH Build Days Aerospace Edition

**Curious about starting a business, but not sure what idea to chase?**

At LAUNCH Build Days, you'll team up for around 32 hours on a real challenge from research or industry, with this edition focused on Aerospace. You can come with friends or build a team on-site.

Over the course of the event, you'll work closely with mentors from research and industry, shape your concept, and finish with a pitch. For the strongest teams, there is also a chance to move on to the LAUNCH Build Labs for six weeks of mentoring and a final Pitch Day. It's free to join, open to students from all disciplines across the Rhine-Main region, and spots are limited. Along the way, you'll leave with a project to show, new connections in the startup scene, and a real taste of what building something from scratch feels like.

## Aerospace

This edition focuses on aerospace and aviation: turning real operational and earth-observation data into decisions that operators can act on. The challenges span airline operations (predicting and explaining flight delay risk in time to act on it) and renewable-asset performance seen from orbit (forecasting long-term wind and solar output from climate projections and satellite data). The common thread is decision intelligence: not a bare model output, but an answer a non-engineer on the desk can trust and use.

## Submission Guide

Here's everything you need to know to hand in your project. Read it once, there are a couple of hard rules you don't want to find out about at 13:59.

**Deadline: Tuesday, 16.06., 14:00 sharp.** One submission per team, no late entries.

Your team number is in Discord. If you're not sure which team you're on, check your team channel.

---

### What you're submitting

Three things, all bundled into one GitHub repo:

1. **A validated problem and customer pair** — who has the problem and how you know it's real.
2. **A solution concept** with a clear value proposition.
3. **A tangible demonstration** — prototype, demo, mockup, simulation, or model. Something the jury can actually look at.

The jury scores you on problem validation, solution quality, prototype tangibility, business potential, and how you present at the expo. Each challenge may add one bonus criterion on top.

For Ready for Takeoff, the expo is a five-minute live demo and pitch followed by four minutes of jury Q&A. Show the working thing, not slides.

### Your repo: the rules

Everything lives in **one public GitHub repo per team.** A few things that aren't negotiable:

- **GitHub only.** Not GitLab, not Bitbucket, not a shared Google Drive folder. If it's not on [github.com](http://github.com), it doesn't count.
- **Public**, so the jury can actually open it.
- **Created during Build Days.** We check the creation timestamp — pre-existing repos are out. The whole point is what you built here.
- **One repo per team.** Pick a teammate to host it on their account, make sure everyone has access.
- Use your team number in the repo name so we can find it — e.g. `team-07-projectname`.

---

### What goes inside

Your README is the jury's landing page. Make it the first thing that's clear. Include:

- Project name and a one-liner on what it does
- Your team number and the names of everyone on the team
- The challenge / track you're tackling
- The problem you're solving and who has it — your validated problem and customer pair
- Your solution and value proposition, in plain language
- A link or pointer to your demo — prototype, mockup, simulation, whatever form it takes

Beyond the README, drop your actual code, prototype files, and anything else the jury might want to look at into the repo. The README is the table of contents — everything should be reachable from there.

### When the commits stop

Once 14:00 hits, **stop pushing.** No new commits until the expo wraps.

We check timestamps. Anything pushed after the deadline can disqualify the entry — the jury reviews what was submitted, not what got polished afterwards, and it has to be fair across all teams.

If you spot a typo at 14:01, breathe and let it go. You built fast, the rough edges are part of the story. Once the expo is over the repo is yours again — push whatever you want.

### How you actually submit

We'll post the submission link (Microsoft Forms) in Discord well before the deadline — keep an eye on the announcements.

When it's live, you'll fill in your team number, your repo URL, and a couple of confirmation fields. Submit early — you can edit the repo right up to 14:00, so there's no reason to wait until the last minute.

### FAQ

**Can the repo be private?** No — has to be public.

**Can we use GitLab / Bitbucket instead?** No. GitHub only.

**Can we use code we wrote before the event?** Open-source libraries, frameworks, and starter templates are fine. Your own pre-existing project code is not.

**Multiple repos?** One main repo per team. Link extras from the README if you have them.

**API keys?** Never commit secrets. Use `.env.example` and add `.env` to `.gitignore`.

**What if we miss 14:00?** The form closes. No late entries.

**Can we keep working on the project after?** Yes — once the expo is done, push whatever you want.

## Challenge

### DATAbility | Ready for Takeoff

**How might we turn a flight's delay risk into a call an operations team will actually act on?**

**Partner.** DATAbility GmbH, an industrial AI and decision-intelligence company spun out of the aerospace sector at TU Darmstadt in 2018. It builds digital twins, predictive maintenance, and decision-intelligence tools for critical infrastructure and transport networks, with an explicit move away from black-box AI toward explainable, trusted engineering solutions.

**The problem.** Thousands of flights are delayed or cancelled every day, costing airlines billions a year. The cost compounds: a late aircraft leaves late on its next leg, crews hit duty limits, passengers miss connections, and one morning delay becomes a dozen by evening. The person who has to catch this early is the dispatcher or duty controller at the operations desk. The catch is that the cause is usually nowhere near the flight: weather closing in on the departure airport, or a knock-on from a delay two legs back. The data that would show it coming exists but is scattered across separate systems, so the desk spends the day reacting. And when a tool does put a number on a flight, controllers often ignore it, because a risk score with no reason attached is just one more thing blinking on a screen.

**The hard part.** A model can say a flight is 60 percent likely to be late. 60 percent is not a decision. The real challenge is less the model and more this: what does the controller do with that number, and what do they need to see to trust it enough to act?

**Constraints (hard boundaries).**

- Build for the industry, not the passenger. Your user works the operation, a named role on the desk, not "the airline" in general. Pick one and build for them.
- No black box. A score that drives a decision has to be explainable to a non-engineer: why a flight is flagged and what is driving it, without reading code.
- Use real flight and weather data. Models and scenarios are expected; the flights, airports, and weather behind a demo are not invented.

**Deliverables.**

1. A sharp customer and problem pair: who exactly makes the decision your tool supports, and what public evidence shows the pain is real. Desk research is enough.
2. A solution concept with a clear value proposition: what you are building, for that user, and why it beats the dashboards-and-gut-feel status quo.
3. A working tool on real data: it takes real flights and outside signals like weather and produces a delay risk with its reasons and a recommended action in view, not a bare score. A narrow, deep slice beats a broad, shallow demo.
4. An expo pitch: five minutes to present and demo, then four minutes of jury Q&A. Show the working thing, not slides.

**Evaluation criteria.**

| Criterion | Description |
| --- | --- |
| Applicability | How realistic and user-friendly is the prototype? Can an operator actually use it for better decision-making? |
| Marketability | Does it solve a high-value problem for airlines or airports? Is the business case and path to implementation convincing? |
| Explainability | Did you apply logical reasoning to the data, and how effectively does the tool communicate why a decision should be made? |
| Pitch and Team | Clear, compelling, confident. The team understands both the operations desk and what the data can and cannot say. |
